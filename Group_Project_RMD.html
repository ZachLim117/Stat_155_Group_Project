Hate Crimes

  • Hate crimes is defined as “any actions of prejudice based on race, religion, sexual orientation, disability, or ethnicity”.

  • In this investigation we will use the package hate_crimes from the fivethirtyeight library. The motivation of this data collection was due to the high report of hate incidents following the 2016 election of President Donald Trump.

  • Looks at how hate crimes varies by states and how might different variables, specifically the median household income and share of population that is non-white, be accountable for this crimes level.

  • Collected from 2 institutions: The Southern Poverty Law Center (Nov 9-18, 2016) and the FBI (2010-2015).

  • The SPLC collect their data based on medias accounts and people’s self-reports, while the FBI collect their based on reports from their agencies.

Research Question:

To what extend does income inequality affect the level of hate crimes reported across the United State?

The objective of this research is to further understand the relationship between the reported hate crimes and the median household income of the state, to perform statistical analysis to confirm or denied our prediction, and to see whether this result is consistent across the two institutions.

The graph above shows similar relationship from the previous graph, showing the relationship between the share of the state population that are not white and the average hate crimes reported to the FBI. This model explains the correlation between average hate crimes and median household income.The intercept coefficient is the mean median household income. The median_house_inc variable explains how much the predicted rates of hate crimes would increase based on increase in household income.

  • Firstly, we made a model that explains the correlation between average hate crimes and median household income. The model includes a coefficient that explains how much the rates of hate crimes would possibly increase based on increase in household income.

\[ \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_I x_I \]

Hypothesis testing

We then accounted for the interaction between median household income and share of non whites. On top of coefficients from the previous model, a new coefficient, median_house_inc:share_non_white, now accounts for the interaction between household income and non white residents in an area. An interesting note is that median household income by itself does not cause a large increase in predicted hate crimes, and increasing non whites causes a significant decrease in predicted hate crimes. However, adding their interaction causes a small overall increase in predicted hate crimes.

Above, we calculated the confidence interval for the predicted effect on rates of hate crimes by the share of non white individuals in an area. * An interesting thing to observe is that there is a decently wide range of possible effect of this coefficient on predicted rates of hate crimes, which is the subject of further investigation later.

Seeing the wide range in confidence intervals in our actual data set, we decided to fit a confidence interval based on 200 bootstrapped samples from the original data set. The reasoning behind modeling the confidence interval for the specific value of share_non_white would be the usefuness of this data in further study. We learned that there is a much larger range in the effect of non white population on rates of hate crimes than previously thought. In further study where we might include data on states and voting in the 2016 election, knowing how much the effect that having non white residents in an area can give us more information on where we can pin down the specific effects on data that the different variables may bring.

Limitations

  1. The median household income data was the 2016 data and was used to predict the hate crimes data from the FBI that was between 2010-2015 only.

  2. Similary, the share of population that is not white was collected in 2015 and was used to explain the hate crime data from the SPLC from 2016 and the FBI data that was an average hate crimes for the 5 years (2010-2015).

  3. The p-value suggests that there might not be a correlation between the 2 chosen explainatory variables and the hate-crime rate. Future investigation could be looking at a range of other variables and their interactive effect(s) on the hate crime rate.
